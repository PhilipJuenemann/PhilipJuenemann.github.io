Project Overview

This project focuses on automatic image colorization using alignment techniques on historical glass plate negatives from the Prokudin-Gorskii collection. The challenge was to align three separate color channel images (Blue, Green, Red) captured through different colored filters to create a single color photograph.

Methodology

1. Initial Approach - Single-Scale Brute Force

The initial "stupid implementation" used a simple brute-force exhaustive search with Euclidean distance to align the color channels. This approach worked reasonably well for smaller images but failed on larger high-resolution images due to computational complexity and alignment accuracy issues.

Results from single-scale brute force alignment showing misalignment issues

2. Image Preprocessing

Cropping (10% border removal)

To improve alignment accuracy, I removed 10% of the image borders to eliminate noisy edges and artifacts that could interfere with the alignment process.

Images after 10% border cropping to remove noise and artifacts

Downscaling for Pyramid Approach

For large images, I implemented a downscaling approach where images are reduced by factor 2 multiple times. This creates an image pyramid that enables coarse-to-fine alignment.

Image downscaled 5 times by factor 2, showing reduced resolution for pyramid alignment

Edge Detection with Sobel Gradients

Instead of aligning raw pixel values, I computed Sobel gradients to detect edges. Aligning based on edge information proved more robust and accurate than pixel-based alignment.

Sobel gradient computation highlighting edges for improved alignment accuracy

3. Final Implementation - Pyramid Alignment

The final approach combines image pyramids with edge-based alignment using Euclidean distance. The algorithm starts at the coarsest level, finds the best alignment, then progressively refines the alignment at finer scales.

Conclusion

The pyramid-based alignment approach with edge detection successfully aligned all images, both small and large. The method proved robust across different image types and lighting conditions. The displacement values show the precise pixel-level adjustments needed to align the color channels, with larger images generally requiring larger displacement corrections due to their higher resolution and more complex alignment challenges.
